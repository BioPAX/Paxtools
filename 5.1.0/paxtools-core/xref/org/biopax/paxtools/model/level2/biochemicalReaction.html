5   /**
6    * A conversion interaction in which one or more entities (substrates) undergo
7    * ovalent changes to become one or more other entities (products). The substrates
8    * of biochemical reactions are defined in terms of sums of species.
9    * This is convention in biochemistry, and, in principle, all of the EC
10   * reactions should be biochemical reactions.
11   *
12   * <b>Examples:</b> ATP + H2O = ADP + Pi
13   *
14   * <b>Comment:</b> In the example reaction above, ATP is considered to be an
15   * equilibrium mixture of several species, namely ATP4-, HATP3-, H2ATP2-,
16   * MgATP2-, MgHATP-, and Mg2ATP. Additional species may also need to be
17   * considered if other ions (e.g. Ca2+) that bind ATP are present.
18   * Similar considerations apply to ADP and to inorganic phosphate (Pi).
19   * When writing biochemical reactions, it is not necessary to attach charges to
20   * the biochemical reactants or to include ions such as H+ and Mg2+ in the
21   * equation. The reaction is written in the direction specified by the EC
22   * nomenclature system, if applicable, regardless of the physiological
23   * direction(s) in which the reaction proceeds. Polymerization reactions
24   * involving large polymers whose structure is not explicitly captured should
25   * generally be represented as unbalanced reactions in which the monomer is
26   * consumed but the polymer remains unchanged, e.g. glycogen + glucose =
27   * glycogen.
28   */
