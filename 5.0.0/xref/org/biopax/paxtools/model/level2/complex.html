5   /**
6    * A physical entity whose structure is comprised of other physical entities
7    * bound to each other non-covalently, at least one of which is a macromolecule
8    * (e.g. protein, DNA, or RNA). Complexes must be stable enough to function as
9    * a biological unit; in general, the temporary association of an enzyme with
10   * its substrate(s) should not be considered or represented as a complex. A
11   * complex is the physical product of an interaction (complexAssembly) and is
12   * not itself considered an interaction.
13   * <b>Comment:</b> In general, complexes should not be defined recursively so
14   * that smaller complexes exist within larger complexes, i.e. a complex should
15   * not be a COMPONENT of another complex (see comments on the COMPONENT
16   * property). The boundaries on the size of complexes described by this class
17   * are not defined here, although elements of the cell as large and dynamic as,
18   * e.g., a mitochondrion would typically not be described using this class
19   * (later versions of this ontology may include a cellularComponent class to
20   * represent these). The strength of binding and the topology of the components
21   * cannot be described currently, but may be included in future versions of the
22   * ontology, depending on community need.
23   *
24   * <b>Examples:</b> Ribosome, RNA polymerase II. Other examples of this class
25   * include complexes of multiple protein monomers and complexes of proteins and
26   *  small molecules.
27   */
