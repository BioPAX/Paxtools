4   /**
5    * <b>Definition:</b> A unification xref defines a reference to an entity in an external resource that has the same
6    * biological identity as the referring entity.
7    * <p>
8    * <b>Rationale</b>: Unification xrefs are critically important for data
9    * integration. In the future they may be replaced by direct miriam links and rdf:id based identity management.
10   * <p>
11   * <b>Usage:</b> For example, if one wished to link from a database record, C, describing a chemical compound in a
12   * BioPAX data collection to a record, C', describing the same chemical compound in an external database, one would use
13   * a unification xref since records C and C' describe the same biological identity. Generally, unification xrefs should
14   * be used whenever possible, although there are cases where they might not be useful, such as application to
15   * application data exchange. Identity of interactions can be computed based on the identity of its participants. An
16   * xref in a protein pointing to a gene, e.g. in the LocusLink database17, would not be a unification xref since the two
17   * entities do not have the same biological identity (one is a protein, the other is a gene). Instead, this link should
18   * be a captured as a relationship xref. References to an external controlled vocabulary term within the
19   * OpenControlledVocabulary class should use a unification xref where possible (e.g. GO:0005737).
20   * <p>
21   * <b>Examples:</b> An xref in a protein instance pointing to an entry in the Swiss-Prot database, and an xref in an RNA
22   * instance pointing to the corresponding RNA sequence in the RefSeq database.
23   */
