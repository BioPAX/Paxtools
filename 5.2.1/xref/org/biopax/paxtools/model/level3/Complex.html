5   /**
6    * <b>Definition</b>: A physical entity whose structure is comprised of other physical entities bound to each other
7    * non-covalently, at least one of which is a macromolecule (e.g. protein, DNA, or RNA). Complexes must be stable enough
8    * to function as a biological unit; in general, the temporary association of an enzyme with its substrate(s) should not
9    * be considered or represented as a complex. A complex is the physical product of an interaction (complexAssembly) and
10   * is not itself considered an interaction.
11   *
12   * <b>Comment</b>: In general, complexes should not be defined recursively so that smaller complexes exist within larger
13   * complexes, i.e. a complex should not be a COMPONENT of another complex (see comments on the COMPONENT property). The
14   * boundaries on the size of complexes described by this class are not defined here, although elements of the cell as
15   * large and dynamic as, e.g., a mitochondrion would typically not be described using this class (later versions of this
16   * ontology may include a cellularComponent class to represent these). The strength of binding and the topology of the
17   * components cannot be described currently, but may be included in future versions of the ontology, depending on
18   * community need.
19   *
20   * <b>Examples</b>: Ribosome, RNA polymerase II. Other examples of this class include complexes of multiple protein
21   * monomers and complexes of proteins and small molecules.
22   */
23  
24  public interface Complex extends PhysicalEntity {
25  
26  
27      /**
28       * Defines the PhysicalEntity subunits of this complex. This property should not contain other complexes, i.e. it
29       * should always be a flat representation of the complex. For example, if two protein complexes join to form a
30       * single larger complex via a complex assembly interaction, the component of the new complex should be the
31       * individual proteins of the smaller complexes, not the two smaller complexes themselves. Exceptions are black-box
32       * complexes (i.e. complexes in which the component property is empty), which may be used as component of other
33       * complexes because their constituent parts are unknown / unspecified. The reason for keeping complexes flat is to
34       * signify that there is no information stored in the way complexes are nested, such as assembly order. Otherwise,
35       * the complex assembly order may be implicitly encoded and interpreted by some users, while others created
36       * hierarchical complexes randomly, which could lead to data loss. Additionally, the physicalEntityParticipants used
37       * in the component property are in the context of the complex, thus should not be reused between complexes.  For
38       * instance, a protein may participate in two different complexes, but have different conformation in each.
39       *
40       * @return components of this complex
41       */
42      Set<PhysicalEntity> getComponent();
43  
44      /**
45       * Defines the PhysicalEntity subunits of this complex. This property should not contain other complexes, i.e. it
46       * should always be a flat representation of the complex. For example, if two protein complexes join to form a

57       * @param component to be added as a new member

92       * The stoichiometry of components in a complex.

98  
99      /**
100      * Gets the member physical entities which are not complex. When the complex is nested, members of inner complexes
101      * are added to the returned set recursively.
102      *
103      * @return non-complex member physical entities
104      */
105     Set<SimplePhysicalEntity> getSimpleMembers();
106 
107     /**
108      * Gets the <code>EntityReference</code>s of the member simple physical entities. When the complex is nested,

112      */
113     Set<EntityReference> getMemberReferences();
